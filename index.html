Section 01

The Signal Flow

The mixer sits at the center of everything. From it, audio splits in three directions: to the speakers in the sanctuary, to the camera for on-card recording, and to the streaming encoder for live broadcast. Get this routing right and the rest of the system becomes predictable.

← swipe to explore →
▲ Why this matters

Many churches plug the camera directly into a separate mic and let the stream pull a different source again. The result: three different audio mixes, none of them good. Routing everything from the mixer means the engineer has one fader to ride, and what the sanctuary hears is what the recording captures and what the stream broadcasts.

Section 02

Working With Your Analog Mixer

You don't need to replace anything to get the full signal chain working. The existing analog mixer can already feed all three destinations — speakers, camera, and stream — as long as the output routing is set up correctly.

  1. Mics & instruments into the mixer
    All wireless mic receivers connect to the mixer via XLR. Instruments use DI boxes if they're high-impedance (acoustic guitar, bass), or line-level inputs for keys. Label every channel on tape across the top of the board.
  2. Main Out → Sanctuary Speakers
    The Main L/R outputs (XLR) feed the powered speakers or amplifier. This is the mix the sanctuary hears. Set the mixer's main fader at unity (0 dB) and use the master knob on the speakers/amp to set room volume.
  3. Aux Out → Camera
    Use a spare Aux Send on the mixer to create a mono mix specifically for the camera. Run an XLR cable from that aux out into the camera's XLR audio input. The camera records this audio directly to its SD card alongside the video — perfectly synced, no post-production required.
  4. Second Aux → Stream encoder
    Use another aux send (or split off the main out with a Y-cable / DI splitter) into the Blackmagic Presenter's XLR audio inputs. The Presenter combines this audio with the camera feed and sends a clean stream to YouTube or Facebook.
  5. Gain staging top to bottom
    Set input gain so the loudest expected signal peaks at −6 dB. Channel faders at unity. Master at unity. This gives headroom and consistency. If you have to push channel faders past +5 to be heard, your gain is too low at the input.
⚠ Watch for ground loops

Connecting the mixer to a camera and a streaming encoder powered from different outlets can introduce a hum. If you hear a 60Hz buzz, use a passive XLR ground-lift / isolation transformer (Radial JDI or similar) on the camera or stream feed.

Section 03

Analog Now, Digital Later

An analog mixer is fine — until it isn't. Here's an honest comparison of where analog hits its ceiling and what a digital mixer unlocks when the church is ready to invest.

Current — Analog

What you have now

  • Physical knobs, immediate tactile control
  • One mix only — what the sanctuary hears is what gets recorded
  • No scene recall — settings reset every Sunday
  • Outboard gear required for EQ, compression, FX
  • No multitrack recording for post-mixing
  • Easy for new volunteers to learn
Upgrade — Digital

What digital adds

  • Save scenes per service, event, or speaker
  • Independent mixes for sanctuary, camera, stream, monitors
  • Built-in EQ, compression, gates, reverb on every channel
  • USB multitrack recording — remix sermons later
  • iPad control — engineer can mix from the floor
  • One unit replaces a rack of outboard gear

▲ Frequency coordination

Running 4+ wireless channels means you must coordinate frequencies. Use Shure's Wireless Workbench or Sennheiser's WSM software (both free) to scan your environment and assign clean frequencies before every service. Two units on the same frequency will cancel each other out.

Section 05

Streaming & SD Recording

The goal: one camera feed, one mixer audio source, two destinations — a high-quality SD card recording AND a live YouTube/Facebook stream. Here's how the chain comes together.

  1. Camera with XLR audio inputs
    A camera with built-in XLR inputs (Sony FX3, Panasonic GH7, Sony A7S III with XLR-K3M handle, or any pro camcorder) records mixer audio straight to the SD card alongside video. This is your master archive — full quality, no streaming compression.
  2. Mixer Aux Out → Camera XLR In
    Run an XLR cable from a dedicated aux send on the mixer to the camera's input. Set the camera's input to LINE level (not mic), disable phantom power on that input, and ride the camera's input gain to peak around −12 dB on the camera meters.
  3. Camera HDMI → Blackmagic Presenter
    The Blackmagic Web Presenter HD takes HDMI video in and outputs USB-C as a webcam to your streaming computer. It also has its own XLR audio inputs — feed it from a separate mixer aux so the stream gets its own dedicated mix (you can boost vocals more for headphone listeners without affecting the room).
  4. Streaming computer → YouTube / Facebook
    OBS Studio (free) or just YouTube Studio's browser streaming sees the Presenter as a webcam. Stream key in, hit go live. The Presenter handles the heavy lifting — your computer just passes the signal through.
  5. After service: pull the SD card
    You now have a perfectly synced video + audio file ready for editing, archiving, or re-uploading as a higher-quality VOD than the live stream captured.
